01 · Overview

What Was Done & Why

Motivation

This project was driven by the recognition that vast amounts of transactional data hold untapped competitive intelligence. Companies across retail — from Walmart to Amazon — leverage data mining to understand customer behavior, manage inventory, and drive targeted marketing.

The central premise: consumers have inertia in their habits, and those habits leave detectable patterns in purchase data. Our goal was to extract those patterns and translate them into actionable business strategy.

Research Questions

Q1. Can we estimate the probability of future purchases by identifying associations between products?

Q2. Are there meaningful insights that emerge from differences in product prices and quantities?

02 · Dataset

The onlineRetail2 Dataset

Sourced from the UC Irvine Machine Learning Repository, this dataset contains transactional records from a UK-based online retailer covering December 2010 through December 2011. The source contains 541,910 rows; our partition includes approximately 400,000 rows after cleaning.

Raw Data

541,910 total rows. Each row represents one line item in an invoice — a single product within a single transaction. Invoice numbers, item descriptions, quantities, prices, and timestamps are included.

What Was Removed

135,080 rows with NA values were dropped. 8,905 rows with negative quantities — likely representing returned items — were also removed. These would distort frequency counts and association probabilities.

Clean Dataset

397,925 rows remain for analysis. This is the foundation for all three analytical phases: association rule mining, price-quantity analysis, and product clustering.

Top Revenue Countries

Exploratory analysis revealed that the United Kingdom, Netherlands, and Eire are the top three revenue-generating countries, with the UK accounting for the overwhelming majority of transactions. Descriptive statistics also indicated peak purchase periods, suggesting seasonality in buying patterns — an important factor for inventory forecasting.

03 · Method

Three Analytical Phases

01
Data Cleaning & EDA
Standardized date columns, trimmed whitespace from descriptions, removed NA values and negative quantities. Followed by descriptive statistics and revenue aggregation by country and time period.
02
Association Rule Mining
Constructed transaction baskets from invoice lines. Ran the Apriori algorithm to find frequent itemsets. Extended the analysis using quantity bins to capture purchase intensity alongside item co-occurrence.
03
Clustering & Pricing
Applied TF-IDF vectorization to item descriptions, then K-Means clustering to group products by linguistic similarity. Separately analyzed price-quantity dynamics for identified complementary items.

Association Rule Mining — The Theory

Association rules — also called market basket analysis or co-occurrence grouping — identify items that appear together in transactions more frequently than chance would predict. The goal is not just to find what co-occurs, but to quantify how surprising and actionable those co-occurrences are.

Three core metrics govern association rule evaluation:

Support
Support(A ∪ B) = freq{A, B} / T

The proportion of all transactions that contain both A and B. Measures how frequently the itemset appears across the entire dataset.

Confidence
Confidence(A → B) = Support(A ∪ B) / Support(A) = p(B | A)

The conditional probability that B appears given A is present. Answers: "When a customer buys A, how often do they also buy B?" A confidence of 90% means 9 out of 10 transactions containing A also contain B.

Lift
Lift(A, B) = p(A, B) / [p(A) × p(B)]

How much more frequently A and B co-occur compared to if they were completely independent. Lift = 1 means no association. Lift > 1 means positive association — they appear together more than chance would predict. Lift < 1 means they repel each other.

Leverage
Leverage(A, B) = p(A, B) − p(A) × p(B)

The difference (rather than ratio) between observed co-occurrence and expected co-occurrence under independence. An alternative to lift that measures absolute rather than relative association strength.

An association is most valuable when it is surprising — when it contradicts expectations or reveals a connection that wouldn't be obvious from domain knowledge alone. Bread and milk appearing together frequently is unsurprising. The pink and green Regency tea plates co-occurring in 90.2% of pink plate transactions is actionable signal.

Apriori Algorithm — Implementation

To prepare for the Apriori algorithm, each invoice was converted from individual item rows into a single transaction basket. This produced 18,536 baskets. The Apriori algorithm was then run with a minimum support threshold of 1% — meaning only itemsets appearing in at least 1% of all invoices were considered frequent.

This threshold produced 275 association rules covering both two-item and three-item sets.

# Build transaction baskets from invoice lines
basket = df.groupby(['InvoiceNo', 'Description'])['Quantity']
           .sum().unstack(fill_value=0)
basket_sets = basket.applymap(lambda x: True if x > 0 else False)

# Run Apriori with min_support = 0.01
frequent_itemsets = apriori(basket_sets, min_support=0.01, use_colnames=True)
rules = association_rules(frequent_itemsets, metric="lift", min_threshold=1)

Extending with Quantity Bins

Standard association rules treat an item as simply present or absent in a transaction — the quantity purchased is ignored. To capture purchase intensity (at what quantity do customers buy related items together?), we extended the analysis using quantity bins.

Each item's quantity was divided into five equal-width bins labeled very small, small, medium, large, and very large. The bin label was attached to the item name, and the Apriori algorithm was re-run on the new baskets. This generated 18 association rules that capture not just whether two items co-occur, but also whether customers tend to buy them in similar or different quantities.

Example finding: In 70.2% of very small orders of pink HBD bunting, there is also a very small order of blue HBD bunting. Lift of 47.48% indicates they co-occur at that order size far more than chance. Conviction of 3.305 implies the inverse (small pink without small blue) occurs almost three times less frequently than if they were independent.

Complementary Item Price-Quantity Analysis

The two items with the greatest lift from the association rules — the Regency Tea Plate Green and Regency Tea Plate Pink — were selected for deeper price-quantity analysis. For each date, total units sold were summed across all invoices and average price was calculated across all invoices for each item. These series were then merged by date and plotted.

Green Plate: Price vs Own Quantity

The green plate quantity remained almost always at the same low level as price increased — an unusual positive price-quantity association, running counter to standard demand theory. This could indicate inelastic demand or bulk pricing effects.

Green Price vs Pink Quantity

As the green plate price increased, the quantity of pink plates also increased. This is the hallmark of a substitute relationship — customers switch toward the pink plate when the green plate becomes more expensive — despite the two being identified as complements through co-occurrence. This nuance demonstrates the value of multi-method analysis.

Product Clustering via TF-IDF + K-Means

To group products into thematic categories, item descriptions were treated as text documents and vectorized using TF-IDF (Term Frequency–Inverse Document Frequency). This converts each item name into a numeric feature vector that reflects which words are distinctive to that item versus common across all items.

TF-IDF Formula
TF(Term, Item) = occurrences of term in item / total terms in item
IDF(Term, Item) = total items in corpus / items containing term
TF-IDF = TF × IDF

Words that appear frequently in a specific item but rarely across the full catalog receive high TF-IDF scores. This means generic words like "RED" or "BAG" score lower than distinctive words like "VINTAGE" or "CHRISTMAS" — even if "RED" appears in that cluster. The TF-IDF matrix has dimensions: rows = number of items, columns = number of unique terms.

# Vectorize item descriptions
items = data["Description"]
vectorizer = TfidfVectorizer()
vec_items = vectorizer.fit_transform(items)
print(vec_items.shape)  # (397925, 2031)

# K-Means with 5 clusters using Euclidean distance on TF-IDF matrix
kmeans = KMeans(n_clusters=5, random_state=42)
results["cluster"] = kmeans.fit_predict(vec_items)

K-Means was chosen from scikit-learn for its ease of use and interpretable centroids. The algorithm uses Euclidean distance on the TF-IDF matrix to determine which items are closest to each cluster centroid, iteratively updating centroids until convergence. The result: items grouped by which distinctive words dominate their descriptions.

05 · Conclusion & Business Strategy

Translating Findings into Decisions

The analysis successfully answered both research questions. Association rule mining confirmed that product co-occurrence patterns are structured and predictable. The Apriori algorithm revealed strong rules — particularly between the pink and green Regency tea plates — enabling confidence-weighted predictions about future purchase behavior. The quantity bin extension added a layer of specificity: not just what customers buy together, but at what scale.

🏷️

Bundle Promotions

The pink and green plate association (90.2% confidence, 61.9× lift) is strong enough to support a direct bundling strategy. A discount on the pair would leverage their natural co-occurrence while providing monetary incentive — likely accelerating a purchase decision that would happen anyway.

🖥️

Online & In-Store Placement

Online platforms should feature the bundle on product pages and potentially the homepage. In physical retail, strategic co-location in the same aisle or display area would reinforce the association at the point of decision.

📦

Inventory Management

Since the items are frequently purchased together, restock orders should be placed in comparable quantities. Descriptive statistics revealed seasonal purchase peaks, meaning inventory decisions should account for forecasted low periods to avoid cash flow disruption from overstocking.

💲

Pricing Dynamics

The finding that green plate price increases correspond to increased pink plate purchases suggests a substitute dynamic despite item co-occurrence. Pricing decisions for one item should account for cross-price elasticity with the other — raising the green plate price may cannibalize green plate revenue while boosting pink plate revenue.

Caution: Overfitting

A key methodological consideration: looking too hard at the data may surface patterns that are artifacts of the specific dataset rather than generalizable signals. All findings here are exploratory — the patterns identified are directionally valid but should be validated against held-out data or future transaction periods before becoming the basis for major strategic commitments.
