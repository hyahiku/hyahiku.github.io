01 · Overview
3.5M Weekly Observations

2-stage Hybrid Retrieval

Climate variability plays a critical role in areas such as agriculture, infrastructure planning, and resource management. This project transforms large-scale Brazilian municipal climate data into interpretable regional profiles, enabling intuitive access through a retrieval-based system.

Using municipal-level climate observations, unsupervised machine learning segments regions into distinct climate profiles based on temperature, humidity, precipitation, and solar radiation patterns. A hybrid retrieval framework then allows users to query this data using natural language — bridging the gap between complex environmental data and non-technical stakeholders.

Research Questions

Q1 · Clustering
How can municipalities be grouped into meaningful climate profiles based on environmental variables?
Q2 · Retrieval
How effectively can natural language queries retrieve municipalities that match specific climate conditions?

03 · Methodology

Three-Stage Pipeline

01
Data Cleaning & Prep
Standardized column values, removed duplicates and missing entries, parsed temporal fields, and eliminated physically impossible values (extreme negatives, impossible humidity). Invalid records totaled only 1.13% of all rows.

Validity was prioritized over row count — both clustering and retrieval are sensitive to distorted inputs.
02
Climate Profile Clustering
Weekly data aggregated to municipality level. Features standardized via StandardScaler before K-Means clustering — essential since each variable operates on a different numerical scale.

Silhouette scores evaluated across multiple values of k. A four-cluster solution was selected for meaningful separation and interpretability over mathematical optimality.
03
Retrieval System
Climate profiles converted to text descriptions using rule-based climate tags, average values, and cluster labels — enabling sentence transformer embeddings.

After semantic-only retrieval failed, a hybrid system was built: structured filtering enforces exact constraints; semantic ranking preserves relevance within the filtered set.

Clustering Detail

Features fed into K-Means: average temperature, temperature range, humidity, precipitation, and solar radiation — all standardized before clustering.

# Standardize features before clustering
from sklearn.preprocessing import StandardScaler
from sklearn.cluster import KMeans
from sklearn.metrics import silhouette_score

features = ['avg_temp', 'temp_range', 'humidity', 'precipitation', 'solar']

scaler = StandardScaler()
X_scaled = scaler.fit_transform(df_muni[features])

# Evaluate silhouette scores for k = 2..10
scores = {k: silhouette_score(X_scaled, KMeans(n_clusters=k).fit_predict(X_scaled))
          for k in range(2, 11)}

# k=4 selected for interpretability + separation
kmeans = KMeans(n_clusters=4, random_state=42)
df_muni['cluster'] = kmeans.fit_predict(X_scaled)
04 · Results

Four Climate Profiles

Clusters were labeled by comparing relative levels across all evaluated features. Interpretation was treated as equally important as statistical performance.

Hot and Dry
High Solar Low Rainfall High Temp

Higher solar exposure and lower rainfall define this group. Characteristic of Brazil's semi-arid interior regions — strong candidates for solar energy development.

Hot, Humid, and Rainy
High Humidity High Rainfall High Temp

Tropical profile with consistently elevated temperature, humidity, and precipitation. Likely corresponding to Amazon basin and coastal tropical zones.

Cool, Humid, and Variable
Low Temp High Rainfall High Variability

Lower temperatures and higher precipitation with notable seasonal variability. Typical of Brazil's southern states, with broader temperature ranges across weeks.

Moderate and Balanced
Mid Temp Mid Humidity Stable

Transitional climate profile with moderate values across all features. Lower variability suggests more stable and predictable seasonal conditions.

Hybrid Retrieval System

Semantic-only retrieval failed initial tests — embedding similarity captures general resemblance but doesn't enforce exact structured conditions. The hybrid approach was developed in response.

Stage 1 · Filter

Municipalities are first tagged by climate thresholds across four dimensions:

hot / moderate / cool humid / moderately humid / dry high / moderate / low rainfall high / moderate / low solar

Structured filtering enforces exact categorical constraints parsed from the natural language query.

Stage 2 · Rank

Within the filtered set, sentence transformer embeddings and cosine similarity rank municipalities by semantic closeness to the query — preserving flexibility and relevance beyond hard constraints.

Outcome

Test queries ("hot and humid cities", "cool regions with high rainfall", "dry and sunny areas") produced results noticeably better aligned with intended climate conditions — especially for multi-attribute queries. This validates combining rule-based filtering with semantic ranking.

Key Finding

For environmental datasets with highly structured underlying data, rule-based interpretation must precede semantic comparison. Semantic similarity alone is insufficient when queries depend on precise categorical conditions — context always determines methodology.

05 · Conclusion

What We Learned

🗺

Clustering

K-Means effectively grouped municipalities into four interpretable profiles statistically separable and geographically plausible across Brazil — confirming that aggregated weekly climate data captures meaningful regional identity.

🔍

Retrieval

Natural language queries can access structured climate insights, but semantic-only retrieval is insufficient for multi-condition queries. The hybrid approach — structured filtering followed by semantic ranking — resolved this limitation effectively.

⚡

Applications

The pipeline provides a foundation for agriculture, renewable energy planning, and climate risk assessment — bridging the gap between complex environmental data and non-technical stakeholders.

📐

Methodology Insight

Interpretation holds equal weight to statistical performance in unsupervised learning. A four-cluster solution was chosen over a higher-scoring k precisely because meaningful separation matters more than optimized scores.
