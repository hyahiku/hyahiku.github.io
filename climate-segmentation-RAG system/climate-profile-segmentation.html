Why This Project

Climate variability plays a critical role in the economy, infrastructure planning, and resource management. Industries like agriculture must align crop selection and irrigation strategies with regional temperatures and precipitation. Solar energy developers prioritize areas with high irradiance and low cloud coverage. Regardless of the organization, climate data provides valuable insight into an environment's state.

This project transforms large-scale climate data into interpretable regional profiles, then makes those profiles accessible through a retrieval-based system — bridging the gap between complex environmental data and non-technical stakeholders.

Research Questions

Q1 · Clustering
How can municipalities be grouped into meaningful climate profiles based on environmental variables?
Q2 · Retrieval
How effectively can natural language queries retrieve municipalities that match specific climate conditions?

03 · Data Cleaning & Preparation

Validity Over Convenience

The cleaning process was guided by one principle: since this project relied on unsupervised learning and retrieval — both of which are highly sensitive to distorted inputs — preserving meaningful data mattered more than maximizing row count.

Figure 1. Three-stage analysis pipeline — Data Cleaning → Clustering → Retrieval System

Step 1 · Standardize Text

All text columns were converted to lowercase and stripped of extra whitespace. This improves consistency and reduces the risk of referencing errors when matching municipality names across operations.

Step 2 · Check Duplicates & Missing Values

The dataset was inspected for duplicate rows and missing values — either of which could skew data quality and invalidate results. Fortunately, the dataset contained neither, confirming completeness from the outset.

Step 3 · Parse ano_semana

The ano_semana column (formatted YYYY/WW) was parsed into separate year and week fields. Although optional for the core analysis, this step preserved the option for future temporal exploration by year or week.

Step 4 · Remove Physically Impossible Values

Descriptive statistics revealed extreme negative temperatures and impossible humidity values. These were not treated as statistical outliers — they fall outside the physically possible range entirely, even beyond extreme natural phenomena. Leaving them would have distorted means, variances, scaling, and clustering behavior. Their removal accounted for only 1.13% of total records.

Step 5 · Assign Logical Value Ranges

After removing impossible values, logical minimum and maximum bounds were assigned to each climate variable. A consistency check then verified that values like temperature fell logically between the recorded minimum and maximum for each observed week — catching any remaining internal contradictions.

Why not treat impossible values as outliers? Statistical outliers exist within the physically possible range — they are unusual but real. Extreme negative temperatures and impossible humidity values don't just exceed expectations, they violate the laws of physics. These are data corruption errors, not anomalies, and require removal rather than capping or winsorizing.

04 · Climate Profile Clustering

From Weekly Observations to Municipal Profiles

Since the project focused on the climate behaviors of each municipality, weekly data was first aggregated to the municipality level. This required careful thought about what "defines" a climate — not just averages, but variability.

Aggregation

Weekly observations were summarized per municipality. Values were averaged, and a temperature range column was manually calculated to capture the spread between maximum and minimum temperatures. Crucially, clusters were not defined by averages alone — conditional fluctuations were also evaluated. Two municipalities may share similar average warmth but differ fundamentally in rainfall stability or humidity variability.

Feature Selection

Five features were used for clustering: average temperature, temperature range, humidity, precipitation, and solar radiation. Together these capture both the central tendency and the variability of each municipality's climate — enabling the algorithm to distinguish between regions that might look similar on one dimension but diverge on others.

StandardScaler

All five features were standardized using StandardScaler before clustering. This was a necessary step since each variable operates on a different numerical scale (e.g., temperature in °C vs. precipitation in mm). Without standardization, features with larger numerical ranges would dominate Euclidean distance calculations and bias cluster formation. Standardization ensured each feature contributed proportionally.

K-Means Selection

K-Means was chosen because it groups based on similarity and produces interpretable cluster centroids. The number of clusters was evaluated using silhouette scores across multiple values of k. Silhouette analysis measures how well each observation fits within its assigned cluster relative to other clusters. Although the highest silhouette score occurred at a larger k, the difference between competing values was modest — so k=4 was selected for meaningful separation while remaining easy to interpret. This illustrates why segmentation decisions should consider interpretability alongside mathematical ranking.

Cluster Labeling

After clustering, centroids were examined by averaging feature values within each cluster. Labels were assigned by comparing the relative levels of all evaluated columns — intentionally reflecting the combination of dimensions that characterize each group, not just the most dominant one.

# Standardize features before clustering
from sklearn.preprocessing import StandardScaler
from sklearn.cluster import KMeans
from sklearn.metrics import silhouette_score

features = ['avg_temp', 'temp_range', 'humidity', 'precipitation', 'solar']
scaler = StandardScaler()
X_scaled = scaler.fit_transform(df_muni[features])

# Evaluate silhouette scores for k = 2..10
scores = {k: silhouette_score(X_scaled, KMeans(n_clusters=k).fit_predict(X_scaled))
          for k in range(2, 11)}

# k=4 selected for interpretability + meaningful separation
kmeans = KMeans(n_clusters=4, random_state=42)
df_muni['cluster'] = kmeans.fit_predict(X_scaled)

05 · Retrieval-Based Query System

Making Climate Data Speak Plain Language

After establishing the climate profiles, the retrieval system was designed to allow non-technical users to query municipalities using natural language. The goal was an accessible tool that provides understandable insights without requiring users to evaluate raw data.

Stage 1 — Text Description Generation

Each municipality's structured climate profile was converted into a natural language text description. This combined three types of information: rule-based climate tags (hot, dry, humid, rainy, high solar exposure), the municipality's average climate measurements, and its assigned cluster label. For example, a description might read: "This municipality is hot and dry, with low solar exposure. Average temperature 28°C. Climate profile: Hot and Dry."

This translation from numerical data to text was necessary because sentence embedding models operate on natural language input. Converting climate characteristics into descriptive summaries places municipalities in a format suitable for semantic search.

Stage 2 — Semantic Embedding

Text descriptions were embedded using a sentence transformer model. Query strings were embedded in the same vector space. Cosine similarity was then used to rank municipalities by closeness to the query. The core idea: municipalities with descriptions semantically similar to the query should receive higher similarity scores.

⚠ Semantic-Only Retrieval Failed — Three test queries were run: "hot and humid cities," "cool regions with high rainfall," and "dry and sunny areas." All three failed. For example, "cool regions with high rainfall" sometimes returned municipalities that were neither cool nor high-rainfall. This occurred because embedding similarity captures general semantic resemblance but does not enforce exact structured conditions. When queries depend on precise categorical constraints, semantic retrieval alone is insufficient.

The Hybrid Solution — Two-Stage Retrieval

This limitation led to an improved hybrid model. Municipalities were first categorically tagged based on climate thresholds across four dimensions:

Stage 1 · Structured Filter

The query is parsed for climate keywords and matched against the categorical tags. Structured filtering enforces exact climate constraints — only municipalities whose tags match the query conditions are included in the candidate set. This ensures precision for categorical queries.

Stage 2 · Semantic Rank

Within the filtered set, sentence transformer embeddings and cosine similarity rank municipalities by semantic closeness to the full query. Semantic ranking preserves flexibility and relevance — it can distinguish nuance within the filtered set that exact tag matching alone would miss.

Outcome

Test queries re-run on the hybrid system produced results noticeably better aligned with intended climate conditions — especially for multi-attribute queries. The combination of rule-based filtering with semantic ranking validated the decision to add the structured layer.

Key lesson: For environmental datasets with highly structured underlying data, rule-based interpretation must precede semantic comparison. Context determines methodology. Semantic similarity is powerful, but it cannot substitute for exact categorical constraints when the query depends on precise conditions. The failed initial test was itself a valuable finding.

06 · Conclusion

What We Learned

Both research questions were successfully addressed through a three-stage pipeline combining data cleaning, unsupervised clustering, and hybrid retrieval.

🗺

Clustering

K-Means effectively grouped municipalities into four climate profiles that were both statistically separable and geographically plausible across Brazil. The inclusion of temperature range as a variability feature — not just averages — was essential in distinguishing profiles that would otherwise overlap.

🔍

Retrieval

Natural language queries can access structured climate insights — but semantic-only retrieval is insufficient for multi-condition environmental queries. The hybrid approach resolved this by combining structured categorical filtering with semantic ranking, producing significantly improved alignment.

📐

Interpretability Over Score

The four-cluster solution was chosen over a higher-scoring k because meaningful, interpretable separation matters more than optimized silhouette scores. Segmentation decisions always require human judgment alongside mathematical metrics.

⚡

Applications

The pipeline provides a foundation for agriculture planning (crop selection by regional climate), renewable energy development (solar irradiance mapping), and climate risk assessment — bridging complex environmental data and stakeholder decision-making.
